<p>A lot of people treat zakat the way they treat New Year's resolutions — good intentions, vague amount, calculated under pressure at the last minute. That's backwards. Zakat is a calculated obligation with real rules, and it deserves the same planning you'd give taxes.</p>

<h2>The basic calculation</h2>
<p>Zakat is generally 2.5% of qualifying wealth that's been held for a full lunar year and exceeds the nisab threshold (based on the current value of gold or silver). Qualifying wealth typically includes:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Cash on hand and in bank accounts</li>
  <li>Gold and silver holdings</li>
  <li>Investment holdings (stocks, funds) at current market value</li>
  <li>Business inventory and receivables</li>
</ul>
<p>Your home, car, and personal-use items generally don't count. Debt you owe is typically subtracted from your zakat base.</p>

<h2>Why "figuring it out later" fails</h2>
<p>If you don't track qualifying assets through the year, the annual calculation turns into a scramble through bank statements and brokerage accounts trying to reconstruct a number. That's how people either underpay out of uncertainty or skip proper calculation entirely and just donate a round number that feels right.</p>

<h2>A simple system that actually works</h2>
<ol>
  <li>Pick one fixed date each year — many people use the start of Ramadan — as your zakat anniversary.</li>
  <li>Keep a running note (a spreadsheet is fine) of qualifying assets updated monthly, not annually.</li>
  <li>On your zakat date, total the qualifying assets, subtract debts owed, apply the nisab threshold check, and calculate 2.5% of what's left.</li>
  <li>Pay it deliberately — treat it as a line item leaving your account, not loose change.</li>
</ol>

<p>Zakat isn't just charity in the Western sense of "give what you can." It's a defined obligation with a defined calculation. Treating it that way isn't cold — it's actually what makes it possible to fulfill it properly, every year, without the annual scramble.</p>
